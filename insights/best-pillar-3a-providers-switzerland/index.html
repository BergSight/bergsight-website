A Comparison of Pillar 3a Providers in Switzerland (2026)

An objective look at every type of provider — from digital fintechs to traditional banks to insurance companies. Who they’re for, what they cost, and what most providers don’t want you to know about fees.

Switzerland has over 90 Pillar 3a providers. Most of them are not worth your time. The market has consolidated around a handful of decent options — and the difference between choosing well and choosing poorly can be worth hundreds of thousands of francs over a 30 or 40 year career.

This article does not accept advertising or referral fees from any provider. The assessments below are based on publicly available fee data, independent comparisons by Moneyland, Handelszeitung, and specialist Swiss finance publications.

Why fees matter more than almost anything else

Before comparing providers, it is worth understanding what fee differences actually mean in practice. The Pillar 3a contribution limit in 2026 is CHF 7,258 per year. Assume you contribute the maximum every year for 30 years, investing in a globally diversified equity portfolio targeting the long-run market return of roughly 6% annually.

CHF 7,258/year contributed over 30 years at 6% gross return — balance after fees
At approximately 0.14% total cost (digital robo-advisor)
CHF 598,000
At 0.39–0.44% total cost (digital fintech provider)
CHF 564,000
At 1.50% total cost (typical actively managed bank fund)
CHF 456,000

The difference between a low-cost digital provider and a typical bank fund is approximately CHF 108,000 over 30 years — on the same underlying market returns. Around 76% of all Swiss Pillar 3a money is held in savings accounts, not invested in securities — the most common and most costly mistake.

The providers in detail

Finpension 3a
Founded 2020 · Independent · App + web
0.39%
All-in annual fee

Finpension launched in 2020 and quickly became a benchmark for digital 3a products. Its foundation is registered in Schwyz — the canton with Switzerland’s lowest pension withdrawal tax at around 4.8%, compared to 10.3% in Basel-Stadt where VIAC is based. The platform invests entirely in index funds (not ETFs), which are more tax-efficient within the Swiss pension system, and does not charge any currency conversion fees.

    Pros

  • Lowest fee among investment-based providers at 0.39%
  • Schwyz foundation — lowest withdrawal tax in Switzerland (~4.8%)
  • Up to 99% equity allocation
  • No FX fees — currency conversion at mid-market rates
  • Up to 5 separate accounts per person for staggered withdrawals
  • Retroactive 3a buybacks supported from 2026
  • Fully customisable portfolio

    Cons

  • App-based initially — web access now available
  • Slightly less mature than VIAC (launched 2020 vs 2017)
  • No cash option — conservative investors may prefer VIAC
  • No insurance or mortgage bundling
Particularly suited to: Long-term investors wanting maximum equity exposure, low fees, and a tax-efficient withdrawal setup. Also worth considering for expats who may withdraw from outside Switzerland, due to the Schwyz foundation domicile.
VIAC
Founded 2017 · WIR Bank · App + web
0.41–0.44%
Typical all-in fee

VIAC launched in 2017 and fundamentally changed the Swiss 3a market by showing that low-cost index investing was possible in pensions. Unlike Finpension, VIAC allows you to hold part of your 3a in cash at WIR Bank, which makes it useful for conservative investors or those closer to retirement. VIAC also offers bundled life and disability insurance — for every CHF 10,000 invested in securities, you receive CHF 2,500 of free coverage. The foundation is in Basel-Stadt, which has higher withdrawal taxes than Schwyz, though this only materialises at withdrawal.

    Pros

  • Established and trusted since 2017
  • Cash option for conservative or short-term investors
  • Free life or disability insurance included
  • Up to 99% equity, full portfolio customisation
  • No fees on the cash portion
  • Mortgage product available through WIR Bank
  • App and web access

    Cons

  • Basel-Stadt foundation — higher withdrawal tax (~10.3%) than Finpension
  • FX spread of 0.75% nominal (VIAC uses netting; effective rate ~0.05%)
  • Very slightly higher fee than Finpension
Particularly suited to: Investors wanting flexibility — including a cash option, insurance bundling, or mortgage integration. Conservative investors approaching retirement who want some capital in cash rather than bonds.
True Wealth
Founded 2014 · Robo-advisor · App + web
~0.14%
Product fees only — 0% management fee

True Wealth charges no management fee on 3a assets — the only costs are the underlying ETF and fund expense ratios, averaging around 0.14% per year, making it the lowest-cost option on a total-cost basis. It is unique in allowing you to manage your 3a and regular investment portfolio within a single account and investment strategy. The foundation is in Basel-Land. One consideration: True Wealth’s business model relies on fees from regular (non-3a) assets — the zero-fee 3a is partly a customer acquisition tool.

    Pros

  • Zero management fee — lowest total cost available
  • Up to 99% equity allocation
  • Integrated with regular investment portfolio in single account
  • ETFs and index funds — fully passive
  • No minimum contribution requirement
  • Strong track record as robo-advisor since 2014

    Cons

  • Basel-Land foundation — moderate withdrawal tax
  • Zero-fee model relies on cross-subsidy from regular assets
  • Less brand recognition than VIAC/Finpension in 3a market
  • Less portfolio customisation than Finpension
Particularly suited to: Existing True Wealth customers who want to manage everything in one place. Also worth considering for cost-sensitive investors with a long investment horizon.
Frankly (ZKB)
ZKB subsidiary · App-based · Zurich foundation
0.44%
All-in annual fee

Frankly is ZKB’s digital 3a app, designed to offer competitive fees while providing the reassurance of a cantonal bank. At 0.44% the fees are competitive and the platform is well-designed. However, Frankly applies a minimum of 70% CHF weighting with currency hedging, which reduces long-term returns by limiting exposure to international currency movements. The maximum equity allocation is also 95%, compared to 99% at some competitors.

    Pros

  • Cantonal bank backing — ZKB is a well-established institution
  • Competitive fee at 0.44%
  • No extra fee for early property withdrawal (WEF)
  • Clean, simple app experience
  • ZKB funds are solid and well-established

    Cons

  • Mandatory 70% CHF / currency hedging — limits long-term return potential
  • Maximum 95% equities vs 99% at Finpension and VIAC
  • Less portfolio transparency than some competitors
  • Zurich foundation — higher withdrawal tax (~7%)
  • Limited customisation relative to Finpension
Particularly suited to: Investors who prefer a cantonal bank product at digital prices. The built-in currency hedging may also appeal to those within 5–10 years of retirement who want to reduce foreign currency exposure.

Traditional bank savings accounts — the comfortable trap

Bank 3a savings accounts at major Swiss banks pay between 0.05% and 0.20% interest annually. The best smaller banks offer up to 1.25%. These accounts make sense in one specific situation: you are planning a property advance withdrawal within one to three years and cannot afford market volatility. For any investor with more than five years until they need the money, a savings account guarantees a loss in real purchasing power.

The 30-year cost

Contributing CHF 7,258/year for 30 years into a savings account at 0.20% leaves you with approximately CHF 238,000. The same contributions invested at 6% annual return leave you with approximately CHF 575,000. The difference — CHF 337,000 — is the cost of not investing. If you currently hold 3a money in a savings account and have more than five years before you need it, transferring to an investment provider is one of the highest-return financial decisions available to you.

If you hold 3a money in a savings account and wish to switch, the receiving provider handles the paperwork. You cannot transfer a partial amount — the entire account must move — but you can open a new investment account and direct future contributions there while leaving any existing savings account unchanged.

Bank pension funds — better than savings, worse than fintechs

Most major banks also offer actively managed pension funds. These are a genuine improvement over savings accounts, but typically charge 0.80–1.80% per year in total costs. Studies consistently show that actively managed Swiss pension funds underperform passive index-based alternatives over the long run while charging significantly more.

Passively managed bank fund options are more competitive, typically around 0.60–0.80%. If you are with a major bank and cannot switch to a fintech provider, these passive options are materially better than their active equivalents.

Insurance 3a — rarely a good option

Insurance-based Pillar 3a products combine retirement savings with life insurance or disability insurance in a single policy. Total annual costs typically run 1.50–2.50% or more, and the structures are often opaque — surrender charges apply if you want to exit early and flexibility is severely limited. Multiple independent Swiss consumer organisations have repeatedly concluded that these products should be avoided by the vast majority of savers.

The one exception

If you have genuine dependants and cannot obtain life or disability cover elsewhere due to health conditions, an insurance-based 3a may be worth considering for the protection element — not the savings element. For everyone else, a separate low-cost term policy plus a digital investment 3a is worth exploring.

Three things most comparisons miss

1. Open multiple accounts

You can hold as many Pillar 3a accounts as you like — the annual contribution limit applies across all accounts combined, not per account. Opening four or five accounts now and distributing contributions across them means you can stagger withdrawals at retirement across multiple years, staying in a lower tax bracket each time. The total tax saving from this strategy can reach 20–40% of your withdrawal tax bill. Both Finpension (up to 5 accounts) and VIAC support multiple accounts.

2. The canton of the foundation matters at withdrawal

Your 3a is taxed at the rate of the canton where the provider’s foundation is registered — not where you live. Finpension (Schwyz, ~4.8%) has among the lowest rates. VIAC (Basel-Stadt, ~10.3%) and Frankly (Zurich, ~7%) are higher. For a CHF 200,000 withdrawal, the difference between Schwyz and Basel-Stadt is roughly CHF 11,000. This matters particularly for expats withdrawing from outside Switzerland.

3. Contribute in January, not December

Most people make their 3a contribution in December to claim the tax deduction. Contributing in January gives your money an additional 11 months of compounding. Over 30 years, this timing difference alone adds a meaningful sum. Set up a standing order at the start of each year.
