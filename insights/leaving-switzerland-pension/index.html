What Happens to Your Pension When You Leave Switzerland?

You have built up Swiss pension savings over years of working here. When you leave, the rules for what you can take with you — and what stays behind — depend almost entirely on one question: where are you going?

Lukas · BergSight April 2026 8 min read

Switzerland's pension system has three pillars. Pillar 1 (AHV state pension) is a pay-as-you-go system — the contributions you made go to current retirees, not into a personal pot. Getting any of that back when you leave is limited. Pillars 2 and 3a are different: those are genuinely your money, sitting in an account with your name on it. But how much you can access depends on where you move to.

Pillar 2 — the occupational pension

Your Pillar 2 balance is split into two portions: the mandatory part (the minimum required by law) and the extra-mandatory part (anything your employer contributed beyond the legal minimum, which is common for higher earners). Your annual pension fund certificate shows both figures clearly.

Moving to an EU or EFTA country — Germany, France, the UK, the Netherlands, Spain, and so on. The mandatory portion must remain locked in a Swiss vested benefits account (Freizügigkeitskonto) until you reach retirement age (currently 65). You cannot access it early. The extra-mandatory portion can be withdrawn as a lump sum at any time after you leave.

Moving outside the EU / EFTA — the USA, Canada, Australia, the UAE, and most of the rest of the world. You can withdraw the entire Pillar 2 balance, both mandatory and extra-mandatory, as a lump sum. No Swiss lock remains.

Important: don't forget vested benefits accounts

Every time you change jobs in Switzerland, your Pillar 2 transfers to a vested benefits account (Freizügigkeitskonto) if there is any gap before your new employer's pension fund kicks in. It is easy to lose track of these accounts over a career. Before leaving, check you have located every account. The Central Office 2nd Pillar (Zentralstelle 2. Säule) can trace lost accounts — contact them at: zentralstelle2saeulen.ch

Pillar 3a — the easiest one

Pillar 3a is straightforward: you can withdraw it when you leave Switzerland permanently, regardless of where you are going. There are no EU / non-EU complications. Contact your bank or investment provider, provide proof of deregistration and your new foreign address, and the funds are typically paid out within four to eight weeks.

One practical consideration: whether to withdraw before or after you leave. If you withdraw while still a Swiss resident, you pay cantonal capital withdrawal tax — a reduced rate, typically 4–8% depending on the canton. If you withdraw after leaving, Swiss withholding tax (Quellensteuer) is deducted at source at the canton where your 3a foundation is registered, and depending on your new country's tax treaty with Switzerland, this may or may not be reclaimable.

The canton matters

Pillar 2 and 3a withdrawals are taxed at the rate of the canton where the pension foundation is registered — not where you live. Schwyz and Zug have among the lowest withdrawal tax rates in Switzerland. If you hold your 3a at a national provider like VIAC or Finpension, check which canton their foundation is registered in before withdrawing. Transferring to a low-tax canton foundation before withdrawal can save you several thousand francs.

Tax on withdrawal — what to expect

Lump-sum pension withdrawals in Switzerland are taxed at a reduced rate, separate from income tax, typically around 4–8% of the total amount depending on the canton. This is applied at source, so you receive the net amount. You will then need to declare the gross withdrawal in your new country of residence — whether it is taxed again there depends on Switzerland's double taxation agreement (DTA) with that country.

Most DTAs give Switzerland the right to tax the withdrawal first, with the new country either exempting it or crediting the Swiss tax paid. A handful of countries — notably France — also apply social contributions on top, which can add a further ~9%. If you are moving somewhere unusual or moving shortly, it is worth checking the specific DTA before you withdraw.

Pillar 1 — AHV contributions

The AHV is not a personal pot. Your contributions fund current retirees. If you move to an EU or EFTA country, Switzerland's bilateral social security agreement means your Swiss AHV years count towards your pension record in your new country — and vice versa. You will eventually receive a proportional Swiss AHV pension at retirement based on your years of contributions here.

If you move to a country without a bilateral agreement, and you contributed for at least one year, you can apply for a partial AHV refund — but only the employee's share of contributions, and only within five years of leaving. It is rarely a large amount but worth applying for if eligible.

Before you leave — the short checklist

1. Get your latest Pillar 2 certificate and note the mandatory vs extra-mandatory split.

2. Trace any vested benefits accounts from previous jobs.

3. Check the canton where your 3a foundation is registered — transfer to a lower-tax canton if the saving is worthwhile.

4. Decide whether to withdraw 3a before or after departure based on your new country's DTA with Switzerland.

5. If moving to the EU / EFTA, confirm the extra-mandatory portion of your Pillar 2 and arrange withdrawal separately.

6. If moving outside EU / EFTA, request the full Pillar 2 withdrawal once you have deregistered.

This article is for informational purposes only. Pension rules and double taxation treaties are complex and change over time. For significant sums, consult a Swiss pension specialist or tax adviser before making any withdrawal decisions.
